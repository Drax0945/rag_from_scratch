The Wright brothers, Orville and Wilbur, were two American aviation pioneers credited with inventing, building, and flying the world's first successful motor-operated airplane. They made the first controlled, sustained flight of a powered, heavier-than-air aircraft on December 17, 1903, four miles south of Kitty Hawk, North Carolina.

Wilbur was born in 1867 and Orville in 1871. Neither brother finished a formal college degree, but both were mechanically inclined from a young age. They ran a bicycle shop in Dayton, Ohio, which gave them the machining skills and small-engine expertise that later proved essential to building an aircraft.

Their key insight, overlooked by many earlier experimenters, was that controlling an aircraft in flight was just as important as generating lift and thrust. They studied how birds bank and turn by twisting the tips of their wings, and applied the same principle to a fixed-wing glider using a system they called "wing warping."

Between 1900 and 1902, the brothers tested a series of gliders at Kitty Hawk, chosen for its steady winds and soft, sandy landing terrain. They built their own wind tunnel back in Dayton to gather more accurate aerodynamic data than what was published at the time, since existing tables from other researchers turned out to be unreliable.

On the morning of December 17, 1903, Orville piloted the first flight, covering 120 feet in 12 seconds. Wilbur made the fourth and longest flight that day, covering 852 feet in 59 seconds. Only five people witnessed the event.

The Wright brothers continued to refine their designs over the following years, and by 1905 they had built the Wright Flyer III, widely considered the first practical airplane, capable of sustained, controllable flight for extended periods.

Their invention transformed transportation and warfare in the twentieth century, laying the groundwork for the modern aviation and aerospace industries.
